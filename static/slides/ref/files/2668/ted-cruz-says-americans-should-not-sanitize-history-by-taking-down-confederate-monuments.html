Ted Cruz says Americans should not 'sanitize history' by taking down Confederate monuments

"I don't think we should go through and simply try to erase from history prior chapters, even if they were wrong," Cruz said during a news conference.

By Gromer Jeffers Jr.

3:33 PM on Aug 17, 2017 CDT

ADDISON — Texas Sen. Ted Cruz said Thursday that cities should not get rid of Confederate statues and monuments in order to "sanitize history."

"I don't think we should go through and simply try to erase from history prior chapters, even if they were wrong," Cruz said during a news conference. "But I think that's a decision each community has to make. ... I suspect different communities will approach this decision differently."

President Donald Trump said Thursday that he was disappointed with the removal of Confederate monuments, saying on Twitter that the nation's cities were losing some of their beauty.

Cruz didn't address Trump's tweets or his sprawling news conference Wednesday, during which Trump seemed to equate neo-Nazis and white supremacists with Americans who protest against them.

The GOP senator has called for a Justice Department investigation into "the acts of domestic terrorism" related to Saturday's neo-Nazi and white supremacist rally in Charlottesville, Va., that resulted in the death of a woman protesting against the rally as well as two police officers.

"It's tragic and heartbreaking to see hatred and racism once again mar our great Nation with bloodshed," Cruz said in a statement released Saturday night after the rally. "The First Amendment protects the rights of all Americans to speak their minds peaceably, but violence, brutality, and murder have no place in a civilized society.

"The Nazis, the KKK, and white supremacists are repulsive and evil, and all of us have a moral obligation to speak out against the lies, bigotry, anti-Semitism, and hatred that they propagate. Having watched the horrifying video of the car deliberately crashing into a crowd of protesters, I urge the Department of Justice to immediately investigate and prosecute this grotesque act of domestic terrorism," the statement said.

On Thursday, Cruz said Americans were still grappling with issues surrounding race and the Civil War.

Dallas Mayor Mike Rawlings called for a task force to study the issue of what to do about the city's public Confederate monuments in Lee Park and Pioneer Plaza. He said he intends to speak with City Council members about how to proceed, but wants the task force to report after 90 days to the Office of Cultural Affairs' board, which in turn will give its recommendations to the council.

Cruz said there are no easy answers.

"It's an agonizing issue for many because it revisits our country's troubled history with race relations and slavery," he said.

Cruz was in Addison to visit executives at Mary Kay Inc. as part of  a swing through Texas during the congressional recess.
